Matcha Museum

A single-page, high-end café style website exploring the history, flavour and design of matcha.

This fictional museum is built as a class project: the layout follows a simple sitemap—Home, History, Types, Brewing, Recipes, and Café—so visitors can wander through each part of the matcha experience.

History

From monastery ritual to café regular.

The History section traces how powdered green tea travelled from Song dynasty China to Japanese tea ceremony, then into the global cafés we recognize now.

1100s · China

Whisked powdered tea appears in Song dynasty court culture, celebrated for its vivid foam and meditative preparation.

1500s · Japan

Zen monks refine powdered tea into chanoyu, a ceremony that turns each bowl into a quiet performance of care and attention.

2000s · Global

Matcha leaves the tearoom and enters bakeries, branding studios and social feeds—keeping its deep green colour but gaining new personalities.

Want the full story? You can read more about matcha on Wikipedia as a starting point.

Types

Grades of green.

Not all matcha is the same. In this section, visitors compare ceremonial, latte, and culinary grades—each with its own ideal use and design personality.

Ceremonial

Usucha (thin tea)

Bright colour, fine texture, and a gentle balance of umami and sweetness. Best enjoyed whisked with water only.

Latte

Everyday café matcha

Slightly more robust to cut through milk and ice. Designed for lattes, frappes, and playful flavour pairings.

Culinary

For baking & desserts

Strong, earthy flavour and darker green colour that stays visible in doughs, batters, and fillings.

Brewing

Whisk, pour, repeat.

Brewing matcha is half science, half choreography. In this “lab” visitors learn how water temperature, ratio, and whisking style shape the final bowl.

  1. Sift 1–2 grams of matcha into a warm bowl.
  2. Add a small splash of cool water to make a smooth paste.
  3. Pour in the rest of your hot water (not boiling).
  4. Whisk in a quick “W” motion until a fine foam appears.

The table on the right acts like a mini brewing cheat-sheet for visitors experimenting with different styles.

Style Matcha Water / Milk Temp
Usucha (thin) 2 g 60–70 ml water 75–80°C
Koicha (thick) 4 g 40 ml water 70–75°C
Hot latte 2 g 180 ml milk 60–65°C
Iced latte 2 g 80 ml water + ice + milk Room temp

*These ratios are a starting point—visitors are encouraged to tweak them based on taste.
